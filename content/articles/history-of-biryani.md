Ask any Indian where biryani comes from and you will get seven different and mutually exclusive answers delivered with complete confidence.

The Hyderabadis will tell you it was perfected by the Nizams. The Lucknawis will tell you the *dum* technique was refined in their city's royal kitchens. The Keralites will point to the Malabar coast and Arab traders. Some food historians argue it predates the Mughals entirely.

All of them are, in some sense, correct.

## The Persian thread

The word *biryani* likely derives from the Persian *birian*, meaning "fried before cooking." Persian rice dishes using layered rice and meat were well-established before the Mughal conquest of India.

## The Mughal contribution

The Mughals did not invent biryani, but they systematized it. The *Ain-i-Akbari*, Abul Fazl's detailed account of Akbar's court, contains recipes that are recognizably biryanis.

## The south Indian argument

Tamil inscriptions from the 2nd century CE describe dishes made with rice and spiced meat that some historians argue are proto-biryanis. The Mappila Muslims of Kerala developed their own distinct tradition through trade with Arab merchants, predating Mughal influence.

The truth: rice-and-meat dishes were being developed simultaneously across multiple culinary traditions, and the Mughal court synthesized one version into what became the canonical form.
