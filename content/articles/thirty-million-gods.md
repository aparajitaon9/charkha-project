The number 330 million — or 33 crore — is often cited as the number of gods in the Hindu tradition. For people outside the tradition, this number lands somewhere between bewildering and absurd.

It is neither. It is a statement about the nature of reality.

## What Hinduism is not

Hinduism is not polytheism in the way ancient Greek religion was polytheistic. The Vedic tradition begins with a more fundamental claim: there is one ultimate reality (*Brahman*), and everything that exists — every god, every human, every atom — is an expression of it.

## What the 330 million actually means

The 33 crore figure comes from the Vedas and originally referred to 33 *categories* of divine principles — cosmic forces governing natural phenomena, aspects of consciousness, principles of existence.

Over time, each principle was given form: a name, a face, a story. This is how a divine principle becomes a *deva* — a deity who can be approached directly.

## Why the proliferation matters

The multiplicity is a feature, not a bug. Every aspect of human experience has a corresponding divine form. A farmer can worship Indra. A musician can approach Saraswati. A merchant can worship Lakshmi. A warrior can approach Durga.

The tradition is not saying these are separate beings. It is saying that the one ultimate reality can be approached through any of these doors.
