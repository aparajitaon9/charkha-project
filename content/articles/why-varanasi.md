Varanasi is the oldest continuously inhabited city on earth. That claim gets thrown around so often it has become inert — a fact that no longer lands. But sit with it for a moment.

While Rome was being founded, Varanasi was already ancient. When the Buddha walked to nearby Sarnath to give his first sermon, Varanasi was already old. It has never not been there.

## What tourists see

The ghats at dawn are genuinely extraordinary. The burning at Manikarnika — where cremations happen around the clock — is unlike anything else on earth. The *aarti* ceremony at Dashashwamedh Ghat is theatrical in the best sense.

None of this is fake. It is all real, ancient, and significant. But it is also the surface.

## What Banarsis live in

The Banarsi relationship with death is not morbid — it is matter-of-fact in a way that takes time to understand. To die in Varanasi is considered auspicious, a release from the cycle of rebirth. This belief is not abstract. People come here to die.

The city has an economy built around death in the most literal sense. But also an economy built around silk, music, and *chaat* — the culture of Varanasi is layered in ways that take more than a few days to appreciate.

## The geography of the sacred

The ghats are not a single thing. There are 88 of them, each with its own history, its own deity, its own purpose. The city behind the ghats — the *galis*, the narrow lanes — is where Varanasi actually lives.

> "Varanasi is older than history, older than tradition, older even than legend, and looks twice as old as all of them put together." — Mark Twain

That is not hyperbole. It is a fair description.
