A dosa is made from two ingredients: rice and lentils. They are soaked overnight, ground into a batter, and left to ferment. The next morning, the batter is spread thin on a hot griddle. It takes about two minutes to cook.

This process is approximately 2,000 years old. The earliest references to dosa appear in Tamil Sangam literature from the 1st century CE.

## The fermentation genius

The overnight fermentation is not just about flavour — it is about nutrition. Rice is low in lysine, an essential amino acid. Lentils are high in lysine but low in methionine. Combined and fermented, they become more nutritious than either ingredient alone.

Whoever developed this dish arrived at a protein-complete food that kept people fed and healthy for two millennia — without biochemistry, without nutritional labels, without the concept of amino acids.

## The diaspora

Today, dosa is eaten across the Indian subcontinent and in every city with a significant South Indian diaspora. It has been adapted into dozens of regional variants — paper-thin Mysore masala dosa, thick set dosa of Udupi, semolina rava dosa.

It has also been adopted by people with no ancestral connection to South India — which is, in the end, the mark of a truly great dish.
