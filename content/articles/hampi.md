In 1520, the Portuguese traveller Domingo Paes visited the city of Vijayanagara and wrote that it was "as large as Rome." He was not exaggerating. At its peak, Vijayanagara — now called Hampi — had a population of between 500,000 and one million people. It was, by some estimates, the second-largest city on earth.

Today it is a UNESCO World Heritage Site surrounded by banana plantations. Most visitors spend one day there. One day is not enough.

## The scale problem

The ruins cover 25 square kilometres. This is not a single archaeological site — it is a landscape of ruined temples, market streets, elephant stables, and sacred complexes spread across ancient boulders.

The Virupaksha Temple at the western end is still active — it has been in continuous use for over a thousand years. The Vittala Temple complex, with its stone chariot and musical pillars, is the set-piece most photographers come for.

The most remarkable thing about Hampi, though, is not any individual monument. It is the cumulative experience of wandering through a city that was once the heart of the last great Hindu empire.

## What ended it

In 1565, a coalition of the Deccan Sultanates defeated the Vijayanagara army at the Battle of Talikota. What followed was systematic destruction. The victorious armies spent months demolishing the city, destroying temples, looting the treasury.

The population fled. The city was never rebuilt.
